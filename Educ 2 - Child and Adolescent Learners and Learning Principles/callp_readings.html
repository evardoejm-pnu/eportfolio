Course Readings

These published materials are essential, evidence-based articles and book chapters that provide the theoretical foundation for the lectures. You were expected to read and reflect on these prior to or during the corresponding class discussion.

    Course Reading 1: The Young Adolescent Learner (Fran Salyers and Carol McKee)
    View Document

    This reading details the distinctive physical, intellectual, social, and emotional characteristics of the 10-15 year old young adolescent learner, emphasizing their need for an educational program specifically designed to address their rapid and complex developmental stage.

    Reflection

    This reading was totally relatable! It explains why middle school is such chaos—you can have an 11-year-old and a 14-year-old in the same room who are worlds apart developmentally. The part about their physical energy making it "uncomfortable" to sit still finally validates why I need to build in movement breaks. It makes lesson planning less about "entertaining them" and more about just respecting how their brains and bodies are actually built right now.

    Course Reading 2: The Importance of Concept of Growth and Development for Educators of All Levels (Dr. Jayaram Kanakala)
    View Document

    Understanding the principles and patterns of human development—distinguishing quantitative growth from qualitative development—is crucial for educators to make appropriate choices, manage student behavior, provide tailored guidance, and identify problems in a student's social, cognitive, and emotional well-being.

    Reflection

    I used to think growth and development were the same thing, so this paper was a huge correction for me—one is just getting bigger (growth), the other is getting better at skills (development). The concept of the spiral pattern really stuck with me. It’s comforting to know that learning isn't a straight line; students circle back and deepen their understanding over time. It gives me a clear blueprint for lesson sequencing, like knowing students need to hear and speak the language before they can really tackle reading and writing it.

    Course Reading 3: The Science of Learning (Deans for Impact)
    View Document

    This document summarizes six core cognitive science principles—focusing on memory, attention, prior knowledge, and practice—to guide educators in developing evidence-based classroom strategies that align with how the human brain naturally processes and retains information.

    Reflection

    This one felt like finally getting the actual owner's manual for the student brain. It blew up all those silly myths like the "learning styles" thing. The biggest takeaway for me is that smart studying isn't about cramming or highlighting; it’s about retrieval practice (quizzing yourself) and spacing out reviews. I’m definitely using these six principles to check my lesson plans, especially to make sure I’m not throwing too much at students at once and totally frying their working memory.

Lecture Handouts

These are core lecture notes and structured overviews created by Dr. Cidro to summarize essential concepts. These documents serve as the primary reference for the theoretical frameworks covered in the lessons.

    Course Handout 1: Human Growth and Development
    View Document

    This handout comprehensively outlines the concepts and principles of development—which is lifelong, multidimensional, and plastic—and summarizes major theories and stages across the lifespan in cognitive, language, socio-emotional, and moral domains from theorists like Piaget, Vygotsky, Erikson, and Kohlberg.

    Reflection

    This handout is a life-saver—it's basically a master cheat sheet for all the big theories we've been reading about! Erikson's Psychosocial Stages resonated the most with me because it reframes student behavior: they're not just acting out, they're navigating an emotional crisis (like Identity vs. Role Confusion). Keeping this chart handy will be super helpful for quickly figuring out a student's developmental stage so I can address the root cause of their behavior, not just the symptom.

    Course Handout 2: Nature, Concepts, and Perspectives of Learning
    View Document

    This handout details seven major learning perspectives—including Behaviorist, Cognitivist, Constructivist, Phenomenology, and Andragogy—by defining their nature, key concepts (like reinforcement, schema, ZPD), foundational theorists, and practical applications in education, all to provide a comprehensive understanding of diverse approaches to teaching and learning.

    Reflection

    This handout is gold for untangling all the different ways we talk about learning. The biggest lightbulb moment was reading about Phenomenology—the idea that it's not just about what I teach, but what the student subjectively takes away from it. It's a great reminder that I can't just stick to one approach. To be a versatile teacher, I need to know when to switch from a Behaviorist technique for managing the class to a Constructivist one for deeper, collaborative learning.

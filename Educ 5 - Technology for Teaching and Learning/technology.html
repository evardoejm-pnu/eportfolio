Teaching Philosophy on Technology

I believe that technology is a powerful and necessary tool for learning, but its potential is strictly limited by the digital divide. My philosophy is that no pedagogy is truly effective if it is inaccessible. Having personally experienced the realities of being a public secondary school teacher, I believe the role of educators is to overcome this divide by finding resourceful and creative ways to implement available technologies for everyone. This maximizes the efficiency of learning while ensuring it remains effective even when technology is scarce. For me, integrating technology is not simply using advanced or 'bleeding-edge' tools, but choosing the right, accessible ones that students can fully utilize and appreciate.

Bridging Evolution and Design

This artifact is the presentation deck of the group reporters for this CILO explaining the historical foundations and evolution of educational technology, from early instructional tools to the human-centered Education 5.0 era. Since we did not have any worksheets or activities for this CILO, I have chosen this presentation because it shows how education has evolved throughout time—moving from a reliance on the teacher's mastery of the subject matter to the present day where education, as I see it, is already decentralized. Synthesis & Final Reflection

The most important takeaway I have gained from this course is the mindful use of technology as an educator. This course has made me pause and reflect on my practice as a college instructor for six years and my seven-month tenure in DepEd: "How do I really use technology in my classes? Do I simply use it as a substitute for my classroom's blackboard or whiteboard and my students' notebooks?" I had always seen technology as a convenience: I could use presentation decks so I wouldn't have to write on the board, and I could conveniently distribute those decks so my students wouldn't have to purchase pens and notebooks. My goal remains to integrate technology into my classes in a way that ensures accessibility for all my students.

CILOs 1 to 3 helped me learn how to map theories onto my learning plans and instructional approaches. I realized how a deliberate planning and injection of technology into my lesson plans can help me improve as a future educator, regardless of the level of education I choose. CILO 2 also helped me realize that aiming to "decentralize" the source of learning means making technology an essential and indispensable requirement for success. CILO 4 grounded the discussion by serving as a strong reminder to be mindful of the ethical and legal grounds of technology; it is not a substitute for actual human teachers. Lastly, by CILO 5, I realized that the instructional media we choose to use should be locally sensitive yet globally responsive.

As a future instructor, I will strive to use technology inclusively by prioritizing accessibility over simply using tools because they are modern or recent. I intend to select tools that are available to all students regardless of socio-economic status, ensuring my pedagogical decisions are fair. By choosing free and open-source software, I can facilitate a learning environment that respects the unique backgrounds of Filipino learners while preparing them for a digital future.

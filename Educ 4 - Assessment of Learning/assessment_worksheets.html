Rethinking Assessment and Learning

Date Submitted: April 2, 2026

In this first activity, I explored the true purpose behind grading and assessments by watching and reflecting on two lecture videos. It helped me understand that tests aren't just about assigning students a number, but about figuring out how to help them learn better.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

During my demo teaching, I worried that asking students about their quiz scores in front of the class might make the lower scorers feel ashamed. However, reflecting on the materials and my resource teacher's advice made me realize that assessments are really a vital feedback loop for my own teaching. Grades shouldn't just be a final measure of performance, but a tool to spot learning gaps so I know how to help. When I know where the students struggle, I can adjust my approach to help them move from basic, everyday learning to actual mastery of a subject. Ultimately, testing is less about judging students and more about guiding my pedagogy.

Short-Term Goal: In my upcoming lessons, I want to start using formative assessments more consistently to gauge the effectiveness of my teaching right away. I plan to use technology to gather this feedback discreetly so that no student feels put on the spot. This will help me quickly identify any gaps and adjust my teaching before moving on to the next topic.
Long-Term Goal: My overall goal is to make item analysis a regular habit so I can identify specific competencies that need addressing. By doing this, I hope to shift away from traditional grading mindsets and focus entirely on using data to design more penetrative and enriching learning experiences. I want to create a classroom culture where assessments are viewed as stepping stones to mastery rather than sources of pressure.

Distinguishing Tests, Measurement, and Evaluation

Date Submitted: January 26, 2026

In this entry, I explored the important differences between tests, measurement, and evaluation in education. Understanding that a test provides a raw measurement, which then allows you to make a fair evaluation, is crucial for effective teaching. Mixing these stages up can easily trap us in a one-size-fits-all grading system that ignores a student's actual progress.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

Looking back at my own high school MAPEH class, I remember how frustrating it was when my grade was dictated purely by raw physical output, like push-up counts. My teacher confused the raw measurement of my strength at that exact moment with a true evaluation of my learning. A fair evaluation should have looked at broader criteria, such as my personal improvement over the term or my effort to master the proper form. When teachers just record scores without using them as diagnostic tools, the assessment turns into a biased administrative chore. This experience really highlighted why educators need to treat these three terms as a structured process to ensure assessments are valid and fair.

Short-Term Goal: My immediate goal is to review my upcoming assessment tools to ensure I am treating tests strictly as instruments to observe behavior. I plan to consciously separate the act of assigning a numerical score from the process of judging a student's worth based on that score. This will help me avoid recording "noisy" data that doesn't reflect my students' true potential.
Long-Term Goal: Ultimately, my goal is to master these concepts so I can consistently design evaluations that account for personal effort and mastery rather than just raw traits. I want to ensure that my assessments provide an honest look at the teaching-learning process. By keeping this hierarchy strict, I hope to always give grades that reflect actual growth rather than a random collection of numbers.

Balancing Assessment as, for, and of Learning

Date Submitted: January 26, 2026

In this activity, I compared traditional and reconfigured assessment pyramids to see how different types of testing impact the classroom. I realized that while traditional methods often focus too much on final grades, a reconfigured approach prioritizes using assessment as a constant tool for student growth.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

My own experience as an educator aligns much more with the reconfigured pyramid, where "Assessment AS" and "FOR" learning form the foundation. I have found that viewing assessment only as a final reflection of achievement, or "Assessment OF" learning, is a missed opportunity. Instead, I prefer to use quizzes and projects as a "pulse check" to monitor how well students are meeting curriculum objectives. If students struggle, I see it as a signal to adjust my own pedagogy rather than a sign of their failure. This data allows me to pivot my teaching style to meet their immediate needs through a continuous feedback loop.

Short-Term Goal: I want to start designing more "Assessment AS" learning activities where students can take the lead in monitoring their own progress. My goal is to use these moments as quick checks to see if I need to change my teaching mid-lesson. This will help me ensure that the data I collect is used immediately to support student growth.
Long-Term Goal: In the long run, I hope to fully move away from the traditional top-down approach where "Assessment OF" learning dominates the classroom. I want to build a teaching practice where the majority of my time is spent on assessments that reinforce learning and self-reflection. By doing this, I can ensure that every evaluation serves as a stepping stone toward student mastery.

Designing Performance-Based Tasks for Flexible Learning

Date Submitted: April 1, 2026

In this collaborative project, my group and I developed a four-week performance task focused on sustainable crop production for Grade 10 TVL-Agriculture students. We designed the activity to be flexible, allowing students to implement a small-scale farming project at home while documenting their progress through digital or written reports.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

Creating this task showed me how powerful performance-based assessments can be when they are grounded in real-world applications. Instead of just reading about farming, students have to actually plan, plant, and solve problems like pest control using sustainable methods. I realized that for a task like this to work in a flexible modality, I must provide very clear weekly guides and measurable outputs so students stay on track. It was also important to ensure the project targets higher-order thinking skills by requiring a final reflection on environmental stewardship. This experience taught me that a well-structured rubric is essential for rating these complex, long-term behaviors fairly.

Short-Term Goal: My goal is to begin integrating more "authentic" tasks into my current lessons that require students to produce tangible outputs rather than just answering multiple-choice questions. I want to practice writing SMART objectives and clear student guides for each task to ensure my instructions are easy to follow regardless of the learning environment.
Long-Term Goal: I aim to master the design of comprehensive performance assessments that seamlessly blend various subject competencies into a single, meaningful project. I want to develop a repository of these real-life tasks that not only test knowledge but also build life skills like accountability and critical thinking. Ultimately, I hope to use these projects to foster a deeper sense of responsibility toward sustainability in all my future students.

Developing Rubrics for Performance-Based Tasks

Date Submitted: January 27, 2026

In this activity, my group and I created a detailed rubric to grade the "Integrated Sustainable Crop Production Project" we designed in the previous worksheet. We focused on making the criteria SMART-specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, and time-bound—to ensure that every part of the student's work is assessed fairly.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

Developing this rubric taught me that a good assessment tool doesn't just give a grade; it provides a roadmap for student success. By defining clear "Outstanding" indicators, I am giving students a clear target to aim for. I realized that weights are just as important as the criteria themselves; giving significant weight to actual demonstrations ensures the focus stays on mastery. This process showed me how a well-structured rubric prevents "noisy" data and keeps evaluations objective. Most importantly, it helps students understand that their grade is based on their growth and the quality of their work.

Short-Term Goal: I plan to start creating simple, clear rubrics for all my upcoming classroom activities so my students always know exactly what is expected of them. My immediate focus will be on writing distinct descriptions for each performance level.
Long-Term Goal: My long-term goal is to become an expert at designing comprehensive rubrics that can accurately measure complex skills like critical thinking. I want to build a collection of these tools that help me provide consistent, high-quality feedback.

Refining this task taught me that a performance activity is only as good as the tool used to grade it. I explored how an ePortfolio acts as a "digital mirror," helping educators bridge the gap between their past experiences as students and their current roles as instructors.

Reflection

Realizing that I lack a "learning diary" from my own foundational years has been a bit of a wake-up call. Long-Term Goal: My ultimate goal is to maintain a comprehensive ePortfolio that serves as a living roadmap for my professional growth. I want to use this collection to show clear evidence of how I meet educational standards.

Building Reliable Instruments for Self-Efficacy

Date Submitted: February 8, 2026

In this activity, I focused on the delicate process of building a scale to measure students' self-efficacy. I learned that creating a reliable instrument requires moving beyond general questions to focus on specific areas like persistence and academic confidence while ensuring the tool is statistically sound.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

I've realized that the most satisfying part of test construction isn't just the design, but the "cleanup" and standardization of the data. By using tools like stanine or sten scores, I can move past simple "pass or fail" labels and truly see which students are struggling with their academic self-belief. A low score often acts as a signal that a student needs more support in building resilience. Ultimately, grounding an assessment in both rigorous statistics and real-world experience creates a bridge to understanding a student's true potential. This human-centered approach to data helps me foster an environment where every student feels they have the power to succeed.

Short-Term Goal: I want to practice applying standardization techniques to my current formative assessments. My goal is to identify at least three students each week who may have the knowledge but lack the self-efficacy to perform well. This will allow me to provide targeted support.
Long-Term Goal: I aim to master the creation of various psychological and academic instruments that are contextually relevant and statistically reliable. I hope to use these tools to build a classroom culture that prioritizes student resilience.

Compass or Filter

Date Submitted: February 15, 2026

This activity focused on shifting the role of testing from a final verdict to a diagnostic tool that guides both teachers and learners. By using techniques like "distracter analysis," I learned how to turn a multiple-choice test into a meaningful conversation about where a student is struggling.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

Reflecting on this approach makes me think back to my high-pressure experience at Parañaque Science High School, where testing often felt like a way to filter students. Failing was treated as a norm of excellence, which caused many of us to lose confidence. To foster true learning, I believe we must combine academic rigor with an encouraging atmosphere where a failed quiz is seen as a roadmap for growth. My goal is to ensure that assessment acts as a compass to navigate learning, making sure every student has the tools to succeed instead of just seeing who survives.

Short-Term Goal: I plan to start incorporating distracter analysis into my post-test reviews. This will allow me to address precise conceptual gaps immediately and turn the checking process into a supportive dialogue.
Long-Term Goal: My ultimate goal is to move away from a "filtering" mindset and establish a classroom culture where high standards are maintained through a diagnostic approach. I want to master the ability to balance rigorous assessments with a holistic environment.

Date Submitted: March 21, 2026

In this activity, my group and I designed a Table of Specifications (TOS) for a Grade 12 Personal Development examination. This tool helped us map out exactly how much time was spent on each lesson and ensured that the number of test items matched the importance of each topic.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

Building a TOS made me realize that a fair exam isn't created by accident; it requires careful planning to make sure no single topic is over-represented or ignored. By calculating the percentage of class time spent on themes, I was able to distribute items logically across different cognitive levels. This process taught me that a test should be a balanced reflection of the actual journey we took in the classroom. It shifts the focus to "validating learning" by ensuring the exam measures the competencies we set out to master. Ultimately, this protects students from "surprise" questions.

Short-Term Goal: I plan to create a simplified TOS for my next unit quiz to ensure alignment with learning competencies. This will help me stay organized and provide students with a clear idea of where to focus.
Long-Term Goal: My goal is to master the use of a TOS for all major examinations to maintain a high standard of validity and fairness. I want to use these blueprints to track student performance trends over time.

Reflection

Academic Integrity in the Age of AI

Date Submitted: April 3, 2026

This activity involved watching a webinar about the evolving state of academic honesty. It explored how we can maintain standards while using modern tools like Artificial Intelligence to support, rather than replace, our own thinking.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

I’ve realized that academic integrity is about being honest with my own growth. While some see AI as a shortcut, I prefer to treat it as a "learning buddy" that helps me organize thoughts when I feel stuck. For me, the true value of education comes from the "heavy lifting" I do myself. By keeping this boundary, I ensure the knowledge gained is genuinely mine. No software can replace the satisfaction that comes from truly mastering a subject through my own effort.

Short-Term Goal: I want to continue using AI tools responsibly by setting strict limits on how much they contribute to my outputs. My goal is to use them primarily for brainstorming so the analysis remains my own.
Long-Term Goal: I aim to model these principles for my future students, showing them how to use technology as a bridge to deeper understanding. I want to create a culture where the learning process is valued as much as the final product.

Seeing Assessment in Action

Date Submitted: March 15, 2026

In this activity, I observed a Grade 8 TLE class focused on the art of table napkin folding. It was a great opportunity to see how a teacher handles real-time coaching and uses practical demonstrations.

View Submitted Document
Reflection

Watching the lesson taught me that the best kind of assessment often happens in the moment. The teacher used "errorless teaching" by giving immediate feedback, which prevented students from practicing a fold incorrectly. Short-Term Goal: In my next practical lesson, I want to practice "active circulating" where I give immediate, verbal feedback to students as they work. My goal is to catch at least one common mistake early on.
